1 Multi-Step Transition Probabilities

1.1 — The \(n\)-Step Probability \(p_n(x,y)\)

In Lecture 1, you learned the one-step transition probability \(p(x,y) = P(X_1 = y \mid X_0 = x)\). But what if you want to know the probability of going from \(x\) to \(y\) in exactly \(n\) steps?

Definition: n-Step Transition Probability (Slide 1)

For a time-homogeneous Markov chain with transition matrix \(p\), define:

\[ p_n(x, y) \;:=\; P(X_n = y \;\mid\; X_0 = x) \]

This is called the \(n\)-step transition probability from \(x\) to \(y\). It equals the \((x,y)\) entry of the matrix \(p^n\) (the transition matrix raised to the \(n\)-th power).

\[ p_n(x,y) \;=\; \bigl(p^n\bigr)_{xy} \]

Why does this work? Intuitively, taking \(n\) steps in a Markov chain is like applying the transition matrix \(n\) times. Each matrix multiplication corresponds to one more step. We'll prove this rigorously using the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation in Section 2.

The n-step transition matrix equals the one-step matrix \(p\) raised to the power \(n\). Entry \((x,y)\) gives \(p_n(x,y)\).

1.2 — Worked Example: Computing \(p_2\)

Take the two-state chain with \(S = \{a, b\}\) and transition matrix:

\[ p = \begin{pmatrix} 1/3 & 2/3 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix} \]

To get \(p_2 = p^2\), multiply \(p\) by itself:

\[ p_2(a,a) = p(a,a)^2 + p(a,b)\,p(b,a) = \tfrac{1}{9} + \tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{4}{9} \]
\[ p_2(a,b) = p(a,a)\,p(a,b) + p(a,b)\,p(b,b) = \tfrac{2}{9} + \tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{5}{9} \]

Check: \(p_2(a,a) + p_2(a,b) = 4/9 + 5/9 = 1\) ✓

2 Chapman-Kolmogorov Equation & the Semigroup Property

2.1 — The Key Identity

The Chapman-Kolmogorov equation tells us how to "stitch together" multi-step transition probabilities. It is the foundation that lets us write \(p_n = p^n\).

Chapman-Kolmogorov Equation (§1.2, Theorem 1.1)

For any states \(x, y \in S\) and any non-negative integers \(n, m\):

\[ p_{n+m}(x, y) \;=\; \sum_{z \in S} p_n(x, z) \cdot p_m(z, y) \]

In matrix form, this is the semigroup property:

\[ p_{n+m} \;=\; p_n \cdot p_m \quad \text{(as matrices)} \]

By induction (taking \(m=1\) repeatedly): \(p_n = p^n\).

2.2 — Visual Intuition: The Intermediate State

To go from \(x\) to \(y\) in \(n+m\) steps, the chain must be somewhere — call it \(z\) — after exactly \(n\) steps. Then it completes the remaining \(m\) steps from \(z\) to \(y\). We sum over all possible intermediate states \(z\):

Chapman-Kolmogorov: to get from \(x\) to \(y\) in \(n+m\) steps, sum over all intermediate states \(z\) at time \(n\).

2.3 — Proof (Step by Step)

Here is the complete proof from the slides, with every step justified. It uses two simple probability rules: (1) \(P(B \cap A) = P(B \mid A)\,P(A)\), and (2) the law of total probability \(P(A) = \sum_{B \in \mathcal{P}} P(B \cap A)\).

Proof of Chapman-Kolmogorov
1
Start with the definition of \(p_{n+m}(x,y)\): \[ p_{n+m}(x,y) \;=\; P(X_{n+m} = y \mid X_0 = x) \]
2
Partition the event by what \(X_n\) (the intermediate state) equals. Sum over all \(z \in S\): \[ = \sum_{z \in S} P(X_{n+m} = y,\; X_n = z \;\mid\; X_0 = x) \] law of total probability: P(A) = Σ P(B∩A), partitioning on X_n
3
Factor using \(P(B \cap A) = P(B \mid A)\,P(A)\): \[ = \sum_{z \in S} P(X_{n+m} = y \mid X_n = z,\; X_0 = x) \cdot P(X_n = z \mid X_0 = x) \] conditional probability rule
4
Apply the Markov property: given \(X_n = z\), the future \(X_{n+m}\) is independent of the past \(X_0 = x\): \[ = \sum_{z \in S} P(X_{n+m} = y \mid X_n = z) \cdot P(X_n = z \mid X_0 = x) \] Markov property: condition on X_n only
5
Use time homogeneity: \(P(X_{n+m} = y \mid X_n = z) = P(X_m = y \mid X_0 = z) = p_m(z, y)\), and \(P(X_n = z \mid X_0 = x) = p_n(x, z)\): \[ = \sum_{z \in S} p_n(x, z) \cdot p_m(z, y) \] time-homogeneous MC definition
6
Recognize this as the \((x,y)\) entry of the matrix product \(p_n \cdot p_m\): \[ = (p_n \cdot p_m)_{xy} \] definition of matrix multiplication
□
💡 Semigroup Property: Why It Matters

The equation \(p_{n+m} = p_n \cdot p_m\) says the family of matrices \(\{p_n : n \geq 0\}\) forms a semigroup under matrix multiplication. Taking \(n=1\) in \(p_{n+m} = p_n p_m\) gives \(p_{m+1} = p_1 p_m = p \cdot p_m\). By induction starting from \(p_0 = I\) (identity), we get \(p_n = p^n\) for all \(n\). This is why we said the \(n\)-step matrix is just the \(n\)-th power of \(p\).

3 Stopping Times, Hitting Times & Return Times

3.1 — What Is a Stopping Time?

A stopping time is a random time \(T\) with a special property: at each moment in time, you can tell whether \(T\) has happened yet — without looking at the future. You can always "stop the clock" just by watching the process unfold.

Definition: Stopping Time (§1.3)

A random variable \(T : \Omega \to \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\} \cup \{\infty\}\) is a stopping time if for every \(n \geq 0\):

\[ \{T = n\} \text{ is determined by } X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_n \]

That is, knowing the values of the chain up to time \(n\) is enough to decide whether \(T = n\). No peeking at \(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, \ldots\)

Why "Stopping Time"?

Imagine watching the chain step-by-step. A stopping time is a rule like: "Stop the first time the chain reaches state 5". You can enforce this rule in real-time as you watch — you don't need to know the future to know when to stop. Contrast this with a non-stopping rule like: "Stop at the last time the chain visits 5 before time 100" — you'd have to wait until time 100 to decide!

3.2 — Hitting Time and Return Time

The two most important stopping times in Markov chain theory are defined for a state \(x \in S\):

Let \(x \in S\) be any state. Define:

\[ T_x \;=\; \min\bigl\{n \geq 0 : X_n = x\bigr\} \quad \text{(hitting time of } x\text{)} \]
\[ T_x^+ \;=\; \min\bigl\{n > 0 : X_n = x\bigr\} \quad \text{(return time to } x\text{)} \]

Convention: \(\min \emptyset = \infty\) (if the chain never reaches \(x\), the time is \(\infty\)).

Hitting Time \(T_x\)

Can equal 0: If \(X_0 = x\), then \(T_x = 0\) (already there!).

Answers: "When does the chain first visit \(x\)?"

Example: If \(X_0 = a, X_1 = b, X_2 = x\), then \(T_x = 2\).

Return Time \(T_x^+\)

Always \(\geq 1\): It counts future returns only (\(n > 0\)).

Answers: "When does the chain come back to \(x\)?"

Note: The textbook (Durrett §1.3) denotes \(T_x^+\) simply as \(T_x\) — your slides point this out explicitly.

Starting at \(X_0 = x\): the hitting time is \(T_x = 0\) (already there). The return time \(T_x^+ = 3\) is the first time \(n > 0\) with \(X_n = x\).
⚠ Why Two Different Times?

This distinction matters when studying whether a chain comes back to where it started. If we used \(T_x\) (which can be 0), starting at \(x\) we'd always have \(T_x = 0\) — trivially! The return time \(T_x^+\) excludes the starting moment, so it meaningfully asks: "does the chain revisit \(x\)?"

4 The Strong Markov Property

4.1 — The Ordinary Markov Property (Recap)

Recall the Markov property from Lecture 1: "the future depends only on the present, not on the past." More precisely, at any fixed time \(n\), the chain "starts fresh" from state \(X_n\).

4.2 — Strong Markov: Markov Property at a Random Time

The strong Markov property extends this to stopping times: the chain starts fresh not just at fixed times, but at any stopping time \(T\).

Strong Markov Property (Slide 10, §1.3 Theorem 1.2)

Let \((X_n)\) be a Markov chain and \(T\) a stopping time. Then conditioned on \(T = n\) and \(X_T = y\), the future process \((X_{T+k})_{k \geq 0}\) behaves like a Markov chain started at \(y\), independent of the past \(X_0, \ldots, X_T\).

\[ P\!\left(X_{T+k} = y_k,\; \forall k \;\Big|\; T = n,\; X_T = y\right) \;=\; P\!\left(X_k = y_k,\; \forall k \;\Big|\; X_0 = y\right) \]

In discrete time: the ordinary Markov property implies the strong Markov property automatically.

4.3 — Why Is This "Strong"?

The ordinary Markov property says: "at fixed time \(n\), the future is independent of the past given \(X_n\)." But what if you want to apply the property at a random time, like the first time the chain hits state 3? That's what "strong" means.

At a stopping time \(T\), the chain "resets." The future is a fresh Markov chain started at \(X_T\), independent of the entire past.

4.4 — Consequence: Why Return Times Are "Geometric"

The strong Markov property has a beautiful consequence. Suppose the chain starts at \(x\) and returns to \(x\). At that return moment, by the strong Markov property, the chain starts fresh from \(x\). So each "excursion" (trip away from \(x\) and back) is independent and statistically identical. This gives us:

Let \(\rho_{xx} = P_x(T_x^+ < \infty)\) be the return probability. By the strong Markov property:

\[ P_x\bigl(\text{return to } x \text{ for the } n\text{-th time}\bigr) \;=\; \rho_{xx}^n \]

So the number of visits \(N_x = \#\{n \geq 0 : X_n = x\}\) starting from \(X_0 = x\) satisfies:

\[ P_x(N_x > n) = \rho_{xx}^n \]

This means \(N_x \sim \text{Geometric}(1 - \rho_{xx})\)

5 Transience and Recurrence

5.1 — The Central Definition

The most fundamental question about any state in a Markov chain: if the chain starts there, will it ever come back? This leads to the transience/recurrence dichotomy.

Definition: Transient vs. Recurrent State (Slide 7, §1.3)

Let \(\rho_{xx} = P_x(T_x^+ < \infty) = P(T_x^+ < \infty \mid X_0 = x)\) be the return probability to state \(x\).

  • Recurrent: \(\rho_{xx} = 1\) — the chain returns to \(x\) with certainty. It visits \(x\) infinitely often with probability 1.
  • Transient: \(\rho_{xx} < 1\) — there is a positive probability \(1 - \rho_{xx} > 0\) of never returning to \(x\). The chain eventually leaves \(x\) forever.

5.2 — Examples from the Slides

Example 1 (Slide 5): Absorbing b

Chain: \(p(a,a) = 1/3\), \(p(a,b) = 2/3\), \(p(b,b) = 1\).

Starting at \(a\): if \(X_1 = a\) (prob 1/3), we're back. If \(X_1 = b\) (prob 2/3), we're stuck in \(b\) forever and never return to \(a\).

So: \(\rho_{aa} = P(X_1 = a \mid X_0 = a) = \mathbf{1/3} < 1\).

State \(a\) is transient.

State \(b\): once you're there, you stay forever. \(\rho_{bb} = 1\). Recurrent.

Example 2 (Slide 5): Two-State Cycle

Chain: \(p(a,b) = 1\), \(p(b,a) = 1\) (deterministic cycle \(a \to b \to a \to \cdots\)).

Starting at \(a\): we go \(a \to b \to a\) with probability 1. So \(T_a^+ = 2\) always.

Therefore \(\rho_{aa} = P_a(T_a^+ < \infty) = \mathbf{1}\).

State \(a\) is recurrent.

5.3 — Geometric Number of Visits

For a transient state, we can say precisely how many times the chain visits it.

If \(x\) is transient (\(\rho_{xx} < 1\)) and \(X_0 = x\), then

\[N_x = \#\{n \geq 0 : X_n = x\} \;\sim\; \text{Geometric}(1 - \rho_{xx})\]

This means:

\[P_x(N_x = k) = \rho_{xx}^{k-1}(1 - \rho_{xx}), \quad k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\]
\[E_x[N_x] = \frac{1}{1 - \rho_{xx}} < \infty\]

Transient states are visited only finitely many times on average.

💡 Intuition via Strong Markov

Think of each return to \(x\) as a "trial." Each trial independently succeeds (chain returns again) with probability \(\rho_{xx}\), or fails (chain leaves forever) with probability \(1 - \rho_{xx}\). This is exactly a geometric distribution! The strong Markov property guarantees the trials are independent — at each return to \(x\), the chain "starts fresh."

5.4 — Recurrence Criterion via Sum of Return Probabilities

There is an elegant equivalent characterization of recurrence (Durrett, Theorem 1.13):

Theorem: Recurrence = Infinite Sum (§1.3, Thm 1.13)

A state \(y\) is recurrent if and only if

\[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} p_n(y, y) \;=\; \infty \]

A state \(y\) is transient if and only if

\[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} p_n(y, y) \;=\; E_y[N_y] \;=\; \frac{\rho_{yy}}{1 - \rho_{yy}} \;<\; \infty \]

Interpretation: the expected total number of visits to \(y\) is infinite (recurrent) or finite (transient).

6 Classification of States

6.1 — Accessibility: Can State \(y\) Be Reached from \(x\)?

Definition: Accessibility (Slide 15, §1.3)

We say that state \(y\) is accessible from \(x\), written \(x \to y\), if:

\[ \exists\, n > 0 : p_n(x, y) > 0 \]

Equivalently: there exists a directed path \(x = z_0, z_1, \ldots, z_k = y\) where each \(p(z_{i-1}, z_i) > 0\). We write \(x \not\to y\) if no such path exists.

Key fact: \(p_k(x,y) \geq \prod_{i=1}^k p(z_{i-1}, z_i) > 0\) if such a path exists (the probability along any one path is a lower bound).

We say \(x\) and \(y\) communicate (\(x \leftrightarrow y\)) if \(x \to y\) AND \(y \to x\). Communication is an equivalence relation — it partitions the state space into communicating classes.

6.2 — Diagram: Transient vs. Recurrent States

Transient states (left, red): the chain can escape to the recurrent class and never return. Recurrent class (right, blue): closed and irreducible — the chain cycles within it indefinitely.

6.3 — The Two Key Claims

Claim 1 (Slide 16 — Proven)

If \(x \to y\) but \(y \not\to x\), then \(x\) is transient.

Proof: Since \(x \to y\), there exists \(n_0\) with \(p_{n_0}(x,y) > 0\). But since \(y \not\to x\), once the chain reaches \(y\), it can never return to \(x\). So \(P_x(T_x^+ = \infty) \geq P_x(X_{n_0} = y) > 0\). Therefore \(\rho_{xx} < 1\), so \(x\) is transient. □

Claim 2 (Slide 16 — Proof Next Lecture)

If \(S\) is finite and \(x \to y \Rightarrow y \to x\) for all \(y\), then \(x\) is recurrent.

Intuition: every state reachable from \(x\) can also reach \(x\) back. The chain "can't escape" to a place with no return path. Full proof deferred to Lecture 3.

6.4 — Decomposition Theorem

For finite state spaces, there is a clean structural result (Durrett, Theorem 1.8):

State Space Decomposition (§1.3, Thm 1.8)

For any Markov chain with finite state space \(S\):

\[ S \;=\; \mathcal{T} \;\cup\; R_1 \;\cup\; R_2 \;\cup\; \cdots \;\cup\; R_k \]

where \(\mathcal{T}\) is the set of all transient states, and each \(R_i\) is a closed irreducible recurrent class (a group of states that all communicate with each other and from which the chain cannot escape).

Starting from a transient state, the chain will eventually enter one of the recurrent classes \(R_i\) and stay there forever.

Concrete Example: Reading the Graph

Consider the chain on \(\{0,1,2,3,4\}\) with transitions: \(0 \to 1\), \(1 \to 2\), \(2 \to 1\) (cycle), \(3 \to 0\), \(3 \to 4\), \(4 \to 4\).

Accessibility: \(3 \to 0 \to 1\), so \(3 \to 1\). But \(1 \not\to 3\) (no path from 1 back to 3). Similarly \(0 \not\to 3\).

Classification: \(\{1, 2\}\) is a closed irreducible recurrent class (cycle). \(\{4\}\) is a closed recurrent class (absorbing state). \(\{0, 3\}\) are transient states.

Decomposition: \(S = \underbrace{\{0, 3\}}_{\mathcal{T}} \cup \underbrace{\{1,2\}}_{R_1} \cup \underbrace{\{4\}}_{R_2}\)

7 Stationary Distributions

7.1 — The Long-Run Question

The last topic introduced in Lecture 2 (Slide 2) asks: in the long run, how much time does the chain spend in each state? This is answered by the stationary distribution.

Definition: Stationary Distribution (§1.4)

A probability distribution \(\pi\) on \(S\) (i.e., \(\pi(x) \geq 0\) for all \(x\) and \(\sum_x \pi(x) = 1\)) is a stationary distribution for the Markov chain with transition matrix \(p\) if:

\[ \pi p \;=\; \pi \]

Written entry-by-entry, for every state \(y \in S\):

\[ \sum_{x \in S} \pi(x)\, p(x, y) \;=\; \pi(y) \]

7.2 — Intuition: The Equilibrium Distribution

Think of \(\pi\) as "steady-state probabilities." If the chain starts in distribution \(\pi\) (i.e., \(P(X_0 = x) = \pi(x)\)), then it remains in distribution \(\pi\) at all future times:

If \(P(X_0 = x) = \pi(x)\) and \(\pi p = \pi\), then:

\[ P(X_n = y) \;=\; \sum_x P(X_0 = x)\,p_n(x,y) \;=\; (\pi \cdot p^n)_y \;=\; \pi_y \]

The distribution doesn't change over time — it's the equilibrium.

💡 Long-Run Time Fraction

For an irreducible recurrent Markov chain, the stationary distribution has the interpretation:

\[\pi(x) \;=\; \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{1}{N} \#\{n \leq N : X_n = x\}\]

regardless of the starting state. So \(\pi(x)\) is the fraction of time the chain spends at \(x\) in the long run — exactly the intuition from Slide 2: "how much of your time is spent at x, in the long run."

7.3 — Computing Stationary Distributions

To find \(\pi\), solve the linear system \(\pi p = \pi\) subject to \(\sum_x \pi(x) = 1\). One of the equations in \(\pi p = \pi\) is redundant (they sum to the same thing), so we replace one with the normalization constraint.

7.4 — Two-State Formula

For the two-state chain with matrix \(p = \begin{pmatrix} 1-a & a \\ b & 1-b \end{pmatrix}\):

\[ \pi(1) = \frac{b}{a+b}, \qquad \pi(2) = \frac{a}{a+b} \]

where \(a = p(1,2)\) and \(b = p(2,1)\) are the "escape" probabilities from each state.

Worked Example

For \(p = \begin{pmatrix} 1/3 & 2/3 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}\): here \(a = 2/3\), \(b = 1/2\).

\(\pi(a) = \dfrac{b}{a+b} = \dfrac{1/2}{2/3 + 1/2} = \dfrac{1/2}{7/6} = \dfrac{3}{7}\)

\(\pi(b) = \dfrac{a}{a+b} = \dfrac{2/3}{7/6} = \dfrac{4}{7}\)

Verify: \(\pi(a) + \pi(b) = 3/7 + 4/7 = 1\) ✓. Check: \(\pi p = \pi\)? \(\pi(a)\cdot(1/3) + \pi(b)\cdot(1/2) = (3/7)(1/3) + (4/7)(1/2) = 1/7 + 2/7 = 3/7 = \pi(a)\) ✓.

7.5 — Existence and Uniqueness (Durrett Theorem 1.14)

Theorem: Unique Stationary Distribution (§1.4, Thm 1.14)

If the transition matrix \(p\) on a finite state space \(S\) is irreducible (i.e., all states communicate: \(x \to y\) for all \(x, y \in S\)), then:

  1. There exists a unique stationary distribution \(\pi\).
  2. \(\pi(x) > 0\) for all \(x \in S\) (no state is completely avoided).
  3. \(\pi(x) = 1 / E_x[T_x^+]\) — the stationary probability equals the reciprocal of the mean return time.
