0 What This Lecture Is About

Lecture 10 takes the basic Poisson process and asks: what happens when we combine processes, randomly keep only some events, label events by type, or attach random jump sizes to arrivals? These are the most useful transformations in the theory.

Superposition

Combine several independent Poisson processes into one bigger process.

Thinning

Take one Poisson process and independently keep each arrival with some probability.

Compound Poisson

At each arrival time, add a random amount instead of adding 1.

Main Theme

Poisson processes are amazingly stable under natural operations. If you merge them or split them in the right way, you often get another Poisson process.

1 Quick Recall: Poisson Process Basics

Definition (Poisson Process)

A counting process \(\{N(t) : t \ge 0\}\) is a Poisson process with rate \(\lambda > 0\) if:

  • \(N(0) = 0\)
  • it has independent increments
  • for any interval of length \(t\), the number of arrivals is Poisson with mean \(\lambda t\):
\[ P\bigl(N(t)=n\bigr) = e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!}, \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \]

This means \(N(t)\) counts random arrivals over time: customers, emails, machine failures, particles detected, and so on.

Small-Interval Picture

Over a tiny time interval of length \(h\):

\[ P(\text{one arrival in }[t,t+h]) = \lambda h + o(h) \]
\[ P(\text{two or more arrivals}) = o(h) \]

This is the idea used in many proofs.

Interarrival Times

If \(T_1, T_2, \ldots\) are the waiting times between consecutive arrivals, then:

\[ T_i \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \operatorname{Exp}(\lambda) \]

So the Poisson process can be viewed by counting arrivals or by adding exponential waiting times.

💡 Mental Model

Poisson process = random arrival stream with constant average rate and no memory.

2 Superposition of Poisson Processes

Superposition Theorem

Suppose \(N_1(t), N_2(t), \ldots, N_k(t)\) are independent Poisson processes with rates \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_k\). Define

\[ N(t) = N_1(t) + N_2(t) + \cdots + N_k(t). \]

Then \(N(t)\) is itself a Poisson process with rate

\[ \lambda = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_k. \]

Why This Is True

If two independent sources each create random arrivals, the combined stream has the total average rate. What is remarkable is that the combined process is exactly Poisson, not just approximately.

Proof via Generating Functions

For a fixed time \(t\), each \(N_i(t)\) is \(\text{Poisson}(\lambda_i t)\). Since the counts are independent, their probability generating functions multiply:

\[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[s^{N(t)}\right] = \prod_{i=1}^k \mathbb{E}\!\left[s^{N_i(t)}\right] = \prod_{i=1}^k e^{\lambda_i t(s-1)} = \exp\!\left\{\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i\right)\!t(s-1)\right\} \]

This is the pgf of Poisson with mean \(\bigl(\sum_i \lambda_i\bigr)t\).

Small-Interval Proof Idea

On a tiny interval \([t, t+h]\):

  • Process \(i\) contributes one arrival with probability \(\approx \lambda_i h\)
  • Two or more arrivals is \(o(h)\)

So the merged process has

\[ P(\text{one arrival}) \approx (\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_k)\,h, \]

exactly the Poisson small-interval rule for rate \(\sum_i \lambda_i\).

Diagram: Merging Two Streams

Merging independent Poisson streams produces a Poisson process with the total rate.
Example: Help-Desk Calls

Calls arrive from students at rate 4 per hour and from staff at rate 3 per hour, independently. The total incoming calls form a Poisson process with rate 7 per hour.

\[ P(N(1) = 5) = e^{-7}\frac{7^5}{5!} \approx 0.1277 \]

3 Thinning (Splitting) a Poisson Process

Setup

Start with a Poisson process \(N(t)\) of rate \(\lambda\). For each arrival, independently label it:

  • Type A with probability \(p\)
  • Type B with probability \(1-p\)

Let \(N_A(t)\) and \(N_B(t)\) count the two kinds of arrivals by time \(t\).

Thinning Theorem

\(N_A(t)\) is a Poisson process with rate \(p\lambda\) and \(N_B(t)\) is a Poisson process with rate \((1-p)\lambda\). Moreover, these two thinned processes are independent.

Intuition

If arrivals come in at average rate \(\lambda\), and each independently survives with probability \(p\), the surviving stream should have average rate \(p\lambda\). The remarkable part is that the surviving process is still exactly Poisson.

Count Argument

Condition on the total count by time \(t\):

\[ N_A(t) \mid N(t) = n \;\sim\; \operatorname{Binomial}(n, p). \]

Mix over the Poisson distribution of \(N(t)\) and the result becomes \(\text{Poisson}(p\lambda t)\).

Small-Interval Viewpoint

In a tiny interval of length \(h\):

\[ P(\text{one A-arrival}) \approx p\lambda h, \]
\[ P(\text{two or more A-arrivals}) = o(h). \]

That is exactly the Poisson rule with rate \(p\lambda\).

Diagram: Thinning

Each arrival is independently routed to Type A (kept) or Type B (rejected). Both sub-streams are Poisson.
Example: Website Purchases

Website visits follow a Poisson process with rate 120 per hour. Each visitor independently buys something with probability \(0.08\). Then purchases form a Poisson process with rate:

\[ 120 \times 0.08 = 9.6 \text{ per hour.} \]

Number of purchases in the next 30 minutes \(\sim \text{Poisson}(9.6 \times 0.5) = \text{Poisson}(4.8)\).

General Splitting

If each arrival is independently labeled type \(1, 2, \ldots, m\) with probabilities \(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_m\) (summing to 1), then the type-\(i\) stream is Poisson with rate \(\lambda p_i\), and all the streams are independent.

4 Conditioning on the Total Count

A very useful trick in Poisson process problems is to condition on the total number of arrivals in a fixed time interval.

Conditional Uniformity Theorem

Given that \(N(t) = n\), the \(n\) arrival times in \([0,t]\) behave like the order statistics of \(n\) i.i.d. \(\text{Uniform}(0,t)\) random variables.

Why This Matters

Once you condition on the total number of arrivals, the exact arrival locations become easy to work with. A Poisson process problem becomes an order-statistics problem.

Equivalent Statement

If \(0 < U_{(1)} < \cdots < U_{(n)} < t\) are the ordered arrival times, then

\[ (U_{(1)},\ldots,U_{(n)}) \mid N(t)=n \]

has the same distribution as the sorted version of \(n\) i.i.d. \(\text{Uniform}(0,t)\) samples.

Type Labels Given Total Count

If each arrival is independently Type A with probability \(p\), then conditional on \(N(t)=n\):

\[ N_A(t) \mid N(t)=n \;\sim\; \operatorname{Binomial}(n,\,p). \]

More generally, type counts have a multinomial distribution.

Conditional on \(N(t)=5\), the 5 arrival times are distributed as 5 ordered uniform points on \([0,t]\).
Example: Bus Arrivals

Buses arrive according to a Poisson process with rate \(\lambda = 2\) per hour. Given that exactly 3 buses arrive in the next hour, what is the probability that at least one arrives in the first 10 minutes?

Conditional on \(N(1)=3\), the 3 arrival times are like 3 i.i.d. \(\text{Uniform}(0,1)\) points.

\[ P(\text{at least one in first }\tfrac{1}{6}\text{ hour} \mid N(1)=3) = 1 - \left(\frac{5}{6}\right)^3 \approx 0.421 \]

5 Compound Poisson Processes

Definition: Compound Poisson Process

Let \(N(t)\) be a Poisson process of rate \(\lambda\), and let \(Y_1, Y_2, \ldots\) be i.i.d. random variables independent of \(N(t)\). Define

\[ S(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{N(t)} Y_i, \]

where the empty sum is 0 when \(N(t)=0\). This is called a compound Poisson process.

Ordinary Poisson counting adds 1 at each arrival. Compound Poisson adds a random jump size instead — modeling random shocks, insurance claims, transaction values, packet sizes, rainfall totals, and many other cumulative quantities.

Important Formulas

Mean
\[ \mathbb{E}[S(t)] = \mathbb{E}[N(t)]\,\mathbb{E}[Y_1] = \lambda t\,\mathbb{E}[Y_1] \]
Variance
\[ \operatorname{Var}(S(t)) = \lambda t\,\mathbb{E}[Y_1^2] \]

Equivalent form:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(S(t)) = \lambda t\,\operatorname{Var}(Y_1) + \lambda t\,(\mathbb{E}[Y_1])^2 \]

Why the Variance Looks Like That

The randomness comes from two sources: the number of jumps \(N(t)\) is random, and the jump sizes \(Y_i\) are random. Using the law of total variance:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(S(t)) = \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\operatorname{Var}(S(t)\mid N(t))\bigr] + \operatorname{Var}\!\bigl(\mathbb{E}[S(t)\mid N(t)]\bigr) \]

Given \(N(t)=n\), \(S(t)\) is a sum of \(n\) i.i.d. terms, so both pieces simplify to give \(\lambda t\,\mathbb{E}[Y_1^2]\).

Sample Path Diagram

Compound Poisson: the process jumps by a random amount \(Y_i\) at each Poisson arrival time, rather than always jumping by 1.
Example: Insurance Claims

Claims arrive as a Poisson process of rate \(\lambda = 5\) per week. Each claim amount has mean \(2000\) and \(\mathbb{E}[Y^2] = 6{,}500{,}000\).

Let \(S(2)\) = total claim amount over 2 weeks \((\lambda t = 10)\):

\[ \mathbb{E}[S(2)] = 5 \cdot 2 \cdot 2000 = 20{,}000 \]
\[ \operatorname{Var}(S(2)) = 5 \cdot 2 \cdot 6{,}500{,}000 = 65{,}000{,}000 \]
⚠️ Key Distinction

Do not confuse \(N(t)\) and \(S(t)\). The first counts how many arrivals occurred. The second accumulates how much total size those arrivals contributed.

6 Worked Examples

Example 1 — Merging Streams

Emails from Team A arrive at rate 6 per hour and from Team B at rate 4 per hour, independently.

(a) What is the distribution of total emails in 30 minutes?

Total rate is \(6+4=10\) per hour. In 30 minutes \((t=0.5)\), mean is \(10 \cdot 0.5 = 5\).

\[ N(0.5) \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(5). \]

(b) What is \(P(N(0.5) = 2)\)?

\[ P(N(0.5)=2) = e^{-5}\frac{5^2}{2!} = \frac{25e^{-5}}{2} \approx 0.0842 \]
Example 2 — Thinning Into Purchases

Customers arrive at a store at rate \(18\) per hour. Each customer independently buys a featured product with probability \(0.25\).

(a) Featured-product purchases form a Poisson process of rate:

\[ 18 \times 0.25 = 4.5 \text{ per hour.} \]

(b) Probability of exactly 3 such purchases in the next 2 hours (mean \(= 4.5 \cdot 2 = 9\)):

\[ P(N_A(2) = 3) = e^{-9}\frac{9^3}{3!} \approx 0.0150 \]
Example 3 — Conditional Count by Type

Suppose 12 arrivals occur in \([0,1]\), and each one is red with probability \(0.3\) independently. What is the probability that exactly 5 are red?

Conditional on the total count being 12, the red count is \(\text{Binomial}(12, 0.3)\):

\[ P(N_R(1)=5 \mid N(1)=12) = \binom{12}{5}(0.3)^5(0.7)^7 \approx 0.1585 \]
Example 4 — Using Conditional Arrival Times

Given that exactly 4 arrivals occurred by time \(t\), what is the probability that all 4 happened after time \(t/3\)?

Given \(N(t)=4\), arrivals are like 4 independent \(\text{Uniform}(0,t)\) points. Each is after \(t/3\) with probability \(2/3\):

\[ P(\text{all 4 after }t/3 \mid N(t)=4) = \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^4 = \frac{16}{81} \approx 0.198 \]
Example 5 — Compound Poisson Expected Total

Machine breakdowns occur as a Poisson process with rate 3 per month. Repair cost per breakdown has mean \(500\) dollars. Expected total repair cost over 4 months:

\[ \mathbb{E}[S(4)] = \lambda t\,\mathbb{E}[Y] = 3 \cdot 4 \cdot 500 = \$6{,}000 \]

7 Common Pitfalls

⚠️ Pitfall 1 — Forgetting Independence

Superposition and thinning formulas rely on independence. If the original streams are not independent, or if labels are not assigned independently, the result need not be Poisson.

⚠️ Pitfall 2 — Mixing Up Rate and Mean

The rate is \(\lambda\) per unit time. The Poisson mean over an interval of length \(t\) is \(\lambda t\). Always multiply by the time length.

⚠️ Pitfall 3 — Thinned Count vs. Conditional Count

Unconditionally, the thinned count over time \(t\) is Poisson with mean \(p\lambda t\). It becomes Binomial only when you condition on the total original count \(N(t)=n\).

⚠️ Pitfall 4 — Poisson Variable vs. Poisson Process

A Poisson random variable gives one count. A Poisson process is the entire time-indexed collection \(\{N(t) : t \ge 0\}\) with independent increments.
