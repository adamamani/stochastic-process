0 What Are We Studying Today?

Lecture 9 studies random events happening over time — such as customers arriving at a store, emails arriving in an inbox, particles decaying, goals being scored, or machines failing. The lecture splits into two complementary views:

Waiting-time view

How long until the next event happens? → Exponential distribution

Counting view

How many events have happened by time \(t\)? → Poisson distribution

Core Message

If events happen at a constant average rate and the future does not depend on how long you already waited, then the waiting time is exponential and the number of events by time \(t\) is Poisson.

1 Counting Processes: The Basic Object

A counting process is a random function \(N(t)\) that records how many events have happened by time \(t\).

\[ N(t) = \text{number of events that have occurred by time } t \]

Any counting process must satisfy these intuitive rules:

  • \(N(0) = 0\): at time 0, nothing has happened yet.
  • \(N(t)\) never decreases: counts only go up.
  • Counts jump by whole numbers: 0, 1, 2, 3, …
A counting process stays flat between events and jumps upward by 1 when an event occurs.

2 Exponential Random Variables

Suppose \(T\) is the waiting time until the next event. The key model in Lecture 9 is:

Definition: Exponential Distribution

\(T \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)\) where \(\lambda > 0\) is the rate (average number of events per unit time). Larger \(\lambda\) means events happen faster, so waits are shorter.

CDF & Survival Function

\(P(T \le t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}\),  \(t \ge 0\)

\(P(T > t) = e^{-\lambda t}\)

Density, Mean & Variance

\(f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}\),  \(t \ge 0\)

\(\mathbb{E}[T] = \dfrac{1}{\lambda}\),   \(\text{Var}(T) = \dfrac{1}{\lambda^2}\)

Interpretation: if \(\lambda = 6\) per hour, the average waiting time is \(1/6\) hour = 10 minutes.

The exponential density starts high at \(t=0\) and decays. Small waiting times are more likely than large ones.
💡 Key Intuition

An exponential waiting time does not mean the event is most likely at one special time. It means the event can happen at any time, but the probability of waiting a very long time decreases exponentially.

3 The Memoryless Property

This is the signature property of the exponential distribution — and the key reason it is so central to stochastic processes.

Memoryless Property

For any \(s, t \ge 0\):

\[ P(T > t + s \mid T > t) = P(T > s) \]

If you have already waited for time \(t\), your remaining wait behaves just like a fresh new wait from the start.

Bus-Stop Interpretation

Suppose buses arrive with exponential waiting times. If you have already been waiting 12 minutes, the probability of waiting at least 5 more minutes is the same as if you had just arrived:

\[ P(T > 17 \mid T > 12) = P(T > 5) \]
⚠️ Do Not Confuse With "Fairness"

Memoryless does not mean the event is "due." The exponential model says the process has no memory of how long you have already waited. The bus is not more likely to arrive just because you've been waiting longer.

Proof of the Memoryless Property

Using the survival function \(P(T > t) = e^{-\lambda t}\):

\[ P(T > t+s \mid T > t) = \frac{P(T > t+s)}{P(T > t)} = \frac{e^{-\lambda(t+s)}}{e^{-\lambda t}} = e^{-\lambda s} = P(T > s) \quad \checkmark \]

4 Races Between Independent Exponentials

What happens when multiple independent exponential clocks run at once? This is called an exponential race. Let \(T_1, \ldots, T_n\) be independent with \(T_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda_i)\).

4.1 — Distribution of the Minimum

Key Result
\[ \min(T_1, \ldots, T_n) \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n) \]

The time to the first event is exponential with rate equal to the sum of all rates. If many events can happen, the race to the first is faster.

4.2 — Which Clock Wins?

\[ P\!\left(T_i = \min(T_1, \ldots, T_n)\right) = \frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n} \]

A faster clock wins the race with probability proportional to its rate.

Whichever clock rings first determines the minimum. Here Clock 2 wins the race.
Example: Two Machines

Machine A fails at rate 2 per day; Machine B fails at rate 5 per day. Then:

\[ P(\text{A fails first}) = \frac{2}{2+5} = \frac{2}{7} \approx 0.286, \qquad P(\text{B fails first}) = \frac{5}{7} \approx 0.714 \] \[ \text{Time to first failure} \sim \text{Exp}(7), \qquad \mathbb{E}[\text{first failure}] = \frac{1}{7} \text{ day} \]
💡 Big Structural Idea

The race idea appears everywhere in stochastic processes: queueing theory, reliability engineering, continuous-time Markov chains, and the Poisson process. Whenever you have competing exponential clocks, the minimum is exponential with the total rate.

5 Sum of i.i.d. Exponential Random Variables

If \(\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots\) are i.i.d. \(\text{Exp}(\lambda)\), define the partial sums:

\[ T_n = \tau_1 + \tau_2 + \cdots + \tau_n \]

This is the waiting time until the \(n\)-th event.

Lecture 9 identifies this as a Gamma (also called Erlang when the shape is an integer) distribution:

Gamma / Erlang Distribution
\[ T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n,\; \lambda), \qquad f_{T_n}(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t} \frac{(\lambda t)^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}, \quad t \ge 0 \]

To wait for the \(n\)-th event you sum \(n\) independent waiting times — so their distribution adds up to Gamma.

Arrival times are partial sums of i.i.d. exponential inter-arrival gaps.

6 Defining the Poisson Process

Now we put everything together.

Definition: Poisson Process with Rate λ

A Poisson process with rate \(\lambda\) is the counting process you get when inter-arrival times are i.i.d. \(\text{Exp}(\lambda)\):

\[ \tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \text{Exp}(\lambda), \qquad T_n = \sum_{i=1}^n \tau_i, \qquad N(t) = \max\{n : T_n \le t\} \]
  • \(\tau_i\) = waiting time between the \((i{-}1)\)-th and \(i\)-th events
  • \(T_n\) = time of the \(n\)-th event
  • \(N(t)\) = count of how many events have occurred by time \(t\)
The Poisson process: arrivals at random times; inter-arrival gaps are i.i.d. Exp(λ).

Equivalent Count-Based Characterization

The same process can be defined directly in terms of counts. A rate-\(\lambda\) Poisson process is a counting process satisfying:

  1. \(N(0) = 0\)
  2. For any \(s, t \ge 0\), the increment \(N(t+s) - N(s)\) has a \(\text{Poisson}(\lambda t)\) distribution: \[ P\bigl(N(t+s)-N(s)=n\bigr) = e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \]
  3. Counts in disjoint time intervals are independent (independent increments).
Two Viewpoints, Same Process

The exponential viewpoint focuses on waiting times between events. The Poisson viewpoint focuses on how many events occur in intervals. Both describe the exact same underlying process.

7 Why Are the Counts Poisson?

One of the nicest intuitions is the small-interval approximation. Break the time interval \([0,t]\) into \(n\) tiny equal pieces. Each piece is so short that it contains 0 or 1 event with high probability, so the count in each piece is approximately Bernoulli:

\[ N_n(t) \approx \text{Binomial}\!\left(n,\; \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right) \]

As \(n \to \infty\), this Binomial converges to \(\text{Poisson}(\lambda t)\) — the classical Poisson limit theorem.

The Binomial-to-Poisson limit explains why event counts in a Poisson process are Poisson distributed.

8 Main Properties to Know

Stationary increments Count over an interval depends only on its length Only the length of a time window matters, not its start

Thinning

If each Poisson event is independently labeled "Type A" with probability \(p\) and "Type B" with probability \(1-p\), then the two sub-streams are independent Poisson processes:

\[ \text{Type A rate} = \lambda p, \qquad \text{Type B rate} = \lambda(1-p) \]

Superposition

If you combine two independent Poisson processes with rates \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\), the merged stream is Poisson with rate \(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2\).

9 Worked Examples

Example 1: Exact Count in a Time Interval

Calls arrive at rate 6 per hour. What is the probability of exactly 3 calls in 30 minutes?

\(t = 0.5\) hours, so \(\mu = \lambda t = 6 \times 0.5 = 3\). Thus \(N(0.5) \sim \text{Poisson}(3)\).

\[ P(N(0.5) = 3) = e^{-3}\frac{3^3}{3!} = \frac{27 e^{-3}}{6} = 4.5\,e^{-3} \approx 0.2240 \]
Example 2: Exponential Race

Alarm A rings after an \(\text{Exp}(2)\) time; Alarm B after an \(\text{Exp}(5)\) time. What is the probability A rings first?

\[ P(\text{A first}) = \frac{2}{2+5} = \frac{2}{7} \approx 0.286 \]

Time to first alarm \(\sim \text{Exp}(7)\), so \(\mathbb{E}[\text{first alarm}] = \tfrac{1}{7}\).

Example 3: Waiting for the 4th Arrival

Customers arrive at rate 3 per minute. What is the expected waiting time until the 4th customer?

\(T_4 = \tau_1 + \tau_2 + \tau_3 + \tau_4\). Each \(\tau_i\) has mean \(1/3\) minute.

\[ \mathbb{E}[T_4] = 4 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{4}{3} \text{ minutes} \approx 80 \text{ seconds} \]
Example 4: Thinning

Emails arrive at rate 12 per hour. Each email is urgent with probability 0.25, independently.

\[ \text{Urgent: Poisson process, rate} = 12 \times 0.25 = 3 \text{ per hour} \]
\[ \text{Non-urgent: Poisson process, rate} = 12 \times 0.75 = 9 \text{ per hour} \]
Example 5: Memoryless Starting Time

If you begin observing a Poisson process at time \(s\), the waiting time from \(s\) to the next event is still \(\text{Exp}(\lambda)\). This follows directly from the memoryless property — the future after time \(s\) does not depend on what happened before \(s\).
